A Case of Colourful Defense

September 27, 2022 | 4 min read

Bacteria can be wildly aesthetically pleasing creatures - many species are found in bright hues, and many others stand out with their brilliance. Still, all of these are never looked into by researchers studying bacterial samples because the colour of the colonies is supposedly as irrelevant as it gets.

George Liu, a biologist at the University of California, San Diego, was no exception. At the time, he was studying two varieties of Streptococcus, a normal one and a mutant variety that failed to produce a toxin. The Liu Lab was curious.

But the experiments were going terribly, filled with inconsistent results. The bacterial samples would sometimes die off for no apparent reason. There were no clear-cut differences in functioning between the standard and mutant varieties other than the said toxin release. It was then that Liu, on just another day in the lab, caught a whiff of bleach from the glassware: the bleach that an enthusiastic undergrad had been using to clean the beakers and flasks but hadn't washed off thoroughly. Liu noticed that the normal strain survived the bleach more than the mutant strain in a serendipitous twist. This introduced the question of whether the toxin was somehow protective against the bleach.

The second light-on moment was at the dinner table when Liu's mother was nagging him about eating brightly-coloured vegetables. Many pigments have antioxidant properties. And 'coincidentally', the typical stain also happened to have bright colouring. Liu hypothesised that it must be the pigment and not the toxin, protecting the normal variety from the bleach.

But how and why would Streptococcus evolve a pigment? And that too, one that protects it from bleach, a recent human invention? But this too was resolved once the fancy chemical name of the active ingredient in bleach was thought about - hypochlorite: the same thing that our immune cells release when they fight against bacterial invaders.

This hypothesis of Streptococcus neutralising the immune system's hypochlorite using its pigment was confirmed by further research.

Following this study on Streptococcus, the Liu lab has tried out the hypothesis on other species and have come up with similar results. For example, the golden colour imparted by carotenoid pigments is the eponymous feature of Staphylococcus aureus, a human pathogen. This distinctive phenotype and its effect on the pathogen's virulence was tested out by Liu and his team in a series of experiments. The results aligned with the expectation: compared to the normal/wild-type bacterium, the mutant variety, with a disrupted synthesis of carotenoid- and an associated lack of pigmentation-is more susceptible to oxidant killing, has an impaired neutrophil survival and is also less pathogenic in a mouse subcutaneous abscess model. The Liu lab had finally proved the aesthetics to matter.
