ANEKDOTA: DNA ORIGAMI IN CANCER TREATMENT

Oct 19, 2022 | 6 min read

Chemotherapy is the leading cancer treatment used today. It involves using cytotoxic agents to attack and kill rapidly dividing cells such as cancer cells. However, administering these drugs to their specific locations can be problematic. These drugs are usually distributed into the circulatory system exposing the whole body to toxicity. Scientists were exploring new ways to find a safe and biocompatible drug delivery system to administer the drug only to tumourstumors, reducing the amount of toxicity to the rest of the body. Recently a group of scientists from Xidian University in China has devised a biosynthetic method of drug delivery using a technique called DNA origami.

Paul Rothenmund developed DNA origami in 2006 to generate structures of different shapes out of DNA. He procured a long piece of single-stranded, viral DNA of about 7200 base pairs. He used a computational approach to design small staples of 20-30 base pair long single-stranded DNA that would randomly bind to the long single-stranded DNA to get the desired shape. Researchers from Xidian University preferred to use DNA origami to establish a new approach to introducing chemotherapy drugs into tumourstumors. This technique has several fundamental attributes that make it favourablefavorable as a drug delivery channel. The short-staple DNA sequences can be engineered to import specific molecules and be designed to hold the drug of interest. DNA origami proved to have a great potential for controlled drug release as it is stable for 12 hours and is degraded within 72 hours. Also, since it was made out of biological material, cells would not recogniserecognize DNA origami as toxic, and drug delivery would be more efficient.

DNA origami is already being used in transporting different molecules like cancer drugs into tissue culture cells. Scientists decided to check if chemotherapy drugs delivered through DNA origami would work in vivo by inducing tumourstumors in a living animal. They generated different sizes and shapes of DNA origami to find the most efficient ones. Researchers injected breast cancer cells just underneath the skin of immune-compromised mice. They also labelledlabeled the breast cells with Green Fluorescent Proteins(GFP) so that they could image the tumourtumor and use total fluorescence to measure tumor size. This makes it easier to monitor tumourtumor volume during drug delivery. They conducted several initial experiments with various forms of DNA origami to determine the most efficient target for tumourstumors. They added quantum dots to DNA origami, allowing scientists to use fluorescent imaging to visualisevisualize their position in the body. They injected DNA origami into the tail vein, which circulated around the mice. Cancer cells tend to take up and retain macromolecules more quickly than other tissues in the body. DNA origami was more likely to target and stay in the tumor. After injecting various forms of DNA origami and imaging quantum dots, they found that triangularly constructed DNA origami was much more efficient in targeting tumourstumors than square or tubular ones. The triangular shape was also almost absent in vital organs. This is important because you don't want to deliver toxic medicines to healthy parts of the body, especially vital organs. The next step was to load the DNA origami with a chemotherapeutic drug called doxorubicin (DOX). Researchers had to test four conditions. They used four groups of mice and injected drug, DOX, drug and DNA origami ( DNA / origami), DNA origami alone, or saline as a control. They injected these solutions every three days and measured the volume and weight of the tumourtumor every three days. The tumor size of the DNA / origami group was significantly reduced to about one-third of its original size in 12 days. Most importantly, DNA / origami treatment showed little toxicity to mice. Exposure to chemotherapeutic drugs usually causes a severe immune response, weight loss, and decreased blood levels. Mice treated with DOX/origami were healthy, especially compared to the DOX-treated group, which lost about 30% of body weight. Mice treated with DOX / Origami also showed no signs of an immune response, and white blood cell and red blood cell counts were completely normal. DNA origami offers a new and effective way to target tumourstumors, given the significant reduction in tumor size and the absence of side effects. It represents a biosynthetic approach to efficiently target tumourstumors with chemotherapeutic agents without appearing to have no harmful side effects. Not only can DNA origami successfully target tumourstumors, but the method of constructing DNA origami can be designed to include imaging molecules so that antitumor effects can be monitored throughout treatment. How technologies like DNA origami, which may have been initially developed for protein biochemical experiments and the development of biomicrocircuits, are being used as incredibly efficient tools in the medical field is exciting.
