The Frozen Graveyard Awakening >

October 22, 2022 | 5 min read

This edition of dark biology comes to you with a different kind of story: unlike our usual horrifying tales of human experimentation and cruelty, this time, our article talks about a frozen graveyard; one that may not remain frozen forever.

It might sound like something from a horror movie featuring zombies, but the following tale of speculative horror is as realistic a fear as it gets.

We have all been hearing the constant and urgent worries of climate change protesters and concerned citizens and environmentalists about how badly global warming can mess our lives and our planet up. Out of the many effects it will have, a new worry has been seeping into scientists’ minds recently: the unleashing of almost-eradicated diseases and viruses from the now-frozen Arctic and Antarctica.

The first twelve days would be so normal you wouldn’t even know to start counting down. And then day 13 hits and bam! Tell-tale signs would start to appear in the form of scabs and scars. If you survive–and 30% do not–you’d be left with scars for life.

Meet smallpox, the Variola major virus, that has been believed to be completely eradicated from the world. Or is it? In 1975, after the last naturally-occurring case of smallpox in a girl in Bangladesh, WHO announced, “The world and its peoples have won freedom from smallpox, which was a most devastating disease sweeping in epidemic form through many countries since earliest time, leaving death, blindness and disfigurement in its wake.” The only further reported cases of smallpox after this were from samples kept in research labs. Once the dangers of this came clear though, even this practice was shut down.

In 1991, a team of Russian bioweapons experts went off trekking near Kolyma River in Siberia, one of the coldest places on the planet, which makes it a perfect site for natural mummification. They had gone to explore this very possibility of smallpox victims buried in the Siberian permafrost. The team brought back samples from the body of a mummified child, and found no hint at all of smallpox. This could be a good sign in that the constant freezing followed by thawing of the cadavers over the hundreds of years may have completely eradicated any traces of viruses that may have been left with them.

To be fair, however, even if the long-gone viruses like smallpox do make a re-appearance with the melting snowcaps, we would still be better off than all the millions who died from it during the first epidemic. Personal and public hygiene has improved by a huge margin, and as a member of the CDC lab(the lab of the trekking group) reported, “No one feels there’s a serious chance that global warming will melt the permafrost and unleash an epidemic.”

But of course, one can never be sure. One cannot prove a negative.
