Reactive Strength Index

June 04, 2021 | 4 min read

The reactive strength index (RSI) illustrates an athlete's capacity to quickly alter from an unconventional movement into a concentric solid compression and is an expression of their energetic vertical bounce capacity. Strength and power skills include peak muscle strength (the amount of strength an athlete can generate regardless of time) and the rate of force development (RFD - how fast an athlete generates strength).

Reactive strength is mainly assessed by the reactive strength index. It is a simple relationship with two measures: how high can you jump? How fast can you jump? The index is calculated by dividing the height reached in the jump by the contact time with the ground. For example, an athlete who jumps 50 cm (0. 5 m) with a contact time of 200 ms (0. 2 s) will achieve an RSI of 2.5 units. RSI can be improved by increasing the jump height or reducing the contact time with the ground.

Another way to test reactive strength is to perform repeated bilateral jump tasks. Ultimately, there are only a few sporting activities in which success depends on a one-time and quick effort of the shortening cycle. The majority of running sports rely heavily on an athlete's ability to repeatedly perform quick and efficient SSC actions. With this in mind, it makes sense to test the reaction force in a repetitive task.

RSI has been shown to be a valid and reliable measure of reactive jumping ability. RSI tests are also often used to measure neuromuscular fatigue during team sports competitions. Testing RSI is an effective way to prescribe exercise load and possibly deal with fatigue. However, you need decent time, knowledge and equipment to achieve a valid score. If you have it at your disposal, you should use it. You can get a good idea or you can perform effective jumps during your plyometric training.

RSI is unique compared to other strength and power abilities. This means that there may be a small correlation between high RSI and strong, fast and powerful athletes. RSI is correlated with the ability to change direction and in sports such as volleyball; the ability to jump high and fast is a key performance indicator. Therefore, the assessment of an athlete's RSI is a good indicator of their plyometric or SSC ability, and since it is important to jump fast and change direction in so many sports, it is a basic test used by many athletes. Reactive strength must be developed through specific training methods, including plyometric training.

Weekly RSI tests are an important anchor point used by coaches to test their training hypotheses. By adding the RSI during normal training to measures of workload or training, we can better assess our athletes and ensure that we deliver peak performance when it matters most. We can also ensure that our athletes do not suffer from maladjustment as non-functional exaggeration.

In summary, RSI can be used to help inform the design of a training program, assess reactive power or plyometric capacity, and track an athlete's readiness and performance fatigue. Here are some tips for using RSI:

  • Evaluate the RSI at the same point every week, for example after a day off.
  • Make sure that the technology and jump test used are not much affected by measurement errors, as this can obscure our ability to detect signal through noise - Plantiga jig systems provide portable and high-performance RSI fidelity comparable to the gold standard, i.e the power plate methodology.
  • Remember, the RSI will not fluctuate like some of the other strength skills - the RSI will naturally decrease during training cycles and with load, and gradually recover as the training load dissipates, so time testing is crucial.
  • Use RSI to assess reaction strength in athletes and identify those who need slower progress in plyometric training, such as: athletes recovering from physical injuries.
