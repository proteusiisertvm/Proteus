Do Animals show empathy?

October 22, 2022 | 5 min read

Empathy is the ability to recognize, understand and share the thoughts and feelings of others. Recent studies show that animals also express empathy for each other.

Evidence suggests that it is more likely in social species or those that live in groups, which means it may have evolved as a way to increase survival. The possibility of compassion in animals presents an entirely different perspective on non-human animals proposing that our inclinations toward them may be reciprocated. It is also possible that they genuinely care for individuals of their own species in a way we humans can relate to. This complex emotional trait has been observed in other primates, as well as dogs, mice, and elephants. Here are some case studies which reflect the empathising character of animals.

Altruistic Rats are an example of empathising animals. Altruism occurs when an individual performs an action that benefits another, often at no benefit or a cost to themself.

A research study conducted in 2011 placed a rat in an enclosure containing a cage-mate trapped in a ‘restrainer’. After some time, the free rat learned to open the cage and release their companion, even without having contact with each other. In a further trial, they found that when given a choice of two restraints, one with a chocolate treat and the other with a companion, the free rat opened both and even shared their reward. This illustrates that rats help a companion in trouble without fundamentally picking up anything.

A 2008 study of elephants collected various behaviors observed on elephants and showed how they provide evidence of empathy. This includes protecting and caring for the cubs of other elephants and their behavior towards injured or dead individuals. One elephant in the study tried to remove the tranquilizer dart from another person. In contrast, the other elephant repeatedly tried to lift and support her dying matriarch and then returned to her body frequently in the next few days. Elephants not related to the dead matriarch also visited the carcass and showed grieving behavior, which shows that grief is not restricted to elephants of the same herd.

Consoling Chimpanzees is compelling evidence of empathy in animals. Consolation, or providing comfort to another individual to make them feel better, has demonstrated compassion in human children. Similar examples of consolation have also been observed in chimpanzee troops. Consolation is thought to reduce stress and repair damaged relationships – this can include behaviours such as offering food and physical contact, for example, grooming or embracing. Like humans, consolation is more likely to occur between socially close individuals than between strangers, and females are more likely to show consoling behaviour than males. In individual bouts, the loser was more likely to obtain consolation if the winner disregarded them afterward, implying that other chimps knew the reasons behind another's desire for comfort.

Researchers claim that consolation is an outstanding example of empathy. A chimpanzee notices another person's misery, feels concerned and tries to alleviate the distress by engaging in behaviours it believes would help. It appears that chimps are not looking for a reward for their consoling behaviour but are just providing comfort like people.

These case studies provide evidence that animals can empathise with members of their species. Some studies also suggest animals are capable of empathising with members of other species, including humans. The response of animals to the environment is very similar to that of humans. They react emotionally to others and even become nervous and anxious when confronted with danger. These emotions have a significant impact on their behaviour. Still, although researchers may never know the actual feelings of animals, studies have found an apparent behavioural similarity between the emotional expressions of animals and humans.
